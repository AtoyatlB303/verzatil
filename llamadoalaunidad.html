“The Black Messiah: A Call for Unity” is a powerful spoken monologue that revives the voice and vision of Fred Hampton — the young revolutionary leader of the Black Panther Party whose dream of solidarity among all oppressed peoples shook the foundations of power. Through gripping narration, immersive sound design, and historical reflection, the piece traces Hampton’s journey from his early activism in Chicago to the creation of the Rainbow Coalition — a movement uniting Black, White, Latino, and poor communities under one banner of justice. It captures his charisma, his revolutionary love for the people, and the fear he inspired in those who ruled through division. As the story reaches its tragic climax — the betrayal and assassination of Fred Hampton at just 21 — the monologue transforms from history into a call to action. It reminds the listener that while revolutionaries can be killed, their ideas live on. This is not just a retelling of one man’s struggle — it is a resurrection of his message. A reminder that unity remains the greatest threat to oppression, and the most powerful force for change.

1. Intro (00:00 - 01:00)

Sonido ambiente: Slow, tense beat builds up. Distant voices chanting “Power to the People.


V.O.
M.R.Lex.

"Let me tell you about someone they don’t teach you about in school. Someone who saw
past race, past division, and called on the people—all people—to stand together. A young
brother, brilliant, fearless, unstoppable. His name? Fred Hampton. And they feared him not
just because he was Black, but because he was uniting Black, Brown, and White in one fight.
They silenced him, but not today. Today, we bring his revolution back.
Fred was not just another leader of his time. He was a vision of what could be. And when
people saw his potential, they saw it not just as a spark of hope, but as a threat to everything
they held dear. So, they did everything in their power to make sure his light was extinguished.
But let me tell you something about revolution—when it's real, it can't be stopped. Not then,
and certainly not now. Fred Hampton’s story is not just his own—it's ours. His fight lives on in
each of us, every time we stand up, speak out, and unite for justice. The world may have tried
to silence him, but today we bring his voice back."


2. Part 1: Early Life and the Fire Within


V.O.
M.R.Lex.


"Fred Hampton was born in 1948, Chicago—the heart of a nation torn by inequality. It wasn’t
just the struggle of Black people that shaped him, but the struggle of anyone who had ever
felt the weight of oppression. From a young age, Fred saw injustice everywhere, but he didn’t
just get angry—he got organized.
Even in high school, Fred wasn’t just another student; he was a leader. He led protests calling
for better schools, better conditions, and fair treatment. This wasn’t just about Black kids
getting an education—it was about dignity for all kids, no matter their race or background.
Fred knew that knowledge was power, so he studied law—not for a paycheck, but to defend
his people, to protect them from a system that was designed to break them down.
Fred’s passion for justice led him to the NAACP, but even then, he saw that speeches and
petitions were not enough. The fight was not just about race—it was about power. It was about

fighting the systems that kept people in poverty, that kept them divided. So, Fred turned his
focus from the status quo to a more radical approach: unity. He understood that you couldn't
just uplift Black people—you had to uplift everyone who was oppressed by the same systems.
This made him a force to be reckoned with, and it’s why they feared him."

3. Part 2: The Rainbow Coalition & The Real Threat


V.O.
M.R.Lex.


"Then came the Black Panther Party. At just 20 years old, Fred Hampton wasn’t just leading
the Chicago chapter—he was building something revolutionary. He was creating the Rainbow
Coalition. A movement that didn’t just focus on one race or one community, but united
everyone. Black, White, Latino, Native, poor folks from every background standing together.
Imagine that. People from all walks of life—coming together to demand justice. And that’s
what scared the system the most. Not just Black people rising up, but everyone uniting against
the real enemy—oppression.
Fred wasn’t just talking about unity—he was showing people how to live it. People who had
been divided for centuries were suddenly standing side by side in a common cause. The
message was clear: you can’t defeat us if we stand together.
But unity is dangerous to the powerful. They knew that if Fred succeeded, if he continued to
bring people together, the whole system could crumble. So, the government did what it
always does when faced with real change: they tried to crush it. They feared Fred Hampton’s
message of unity because it threatened their control."
Sound: Government radio chatter, FBI documents being shuffled.
Narrator:
"They didn’t just see the Black Panthers as a threat—they saw unity itself as a threat.
COINTELPRO, J. Edgar Hoover, the FBI—they weren’t just trying to stop Black leaders.
They were trying to stop the people from realizing their own power. Because once we
understand that power? We stop playing by their rules. And that is a revolution they couldn’t
allow."

4. Part 3: Betrayal and The Night They Stole the Future


V.O.
M.R.Lex.


"On December 4th, 1969, Fred Hampton was asleep in his bed, just 21 years old. He was
young, he was gifted, and his vision for the world was more alive than ever. But there were
forces that wanted to make sure that vision never saw the light of day.

An informant—William O’Neal—was planted within Fred’s inner circle. O'Neal gained Fred's
trust and ultimately betrayed him, feeding crucial information to the police. That night, while
Fred slept, the Chicago police stormed in, armed with rifles and automatic weapons. There
were no warnings, no arrests—just bullets. Over 90 rounds were fired into that apartment.
Fred never had a chance to defend himself.
It wasn’t a shootout. It was an assassination."
Sound: Sudden gunfire, chaos, silence.
Narrator (somber, reflective):
"They killed Fred Hampton in cold blood. They called it a ‘shootout,’ but Fred never even
fired a shot. He was lying in his bed, drugged, asleep. And in that moment, they stole the
future—the future of a united people, a revolution that could have changed everything. They
took his life, but they didn’t take his spirit. They couldn’t. Fred Hampton was more than just
a man. He was an idea. A dream. And that’s why they had to kill him."

5. Outro: The Revolution Lives On


V.O.
M.R.Lex.

"Fred Hampton didn’t just fight for one group. He fought for all of us. He fought for the
poor, for the disenfranchised, for those who had been silenced for far too long. His vision
wasn’t just about Black liberation—it was about liberation for everyone who has ever been
crushed under the weight of oppression. He believed in something bigger than himself, and
that’s why they feared him.
But the truth is, they can’t kill unity. They can’t kill the idea that we are stronger together.
Fred Hampton’s message, his dream, is still alive. It lives in every protest, every act of
resistance, every moment when people come together to demand change.
They tried to erase his name from history. They tried to bury his revolution. But we bring his
name back, we bring his fight back. We carry his dream with us.
Remember Fred Hampton. Remember the Rainbow Coalition. Remember the dream of
unity, because it’s not over. The revolution is alive, and it will never be silenced."
Sound: Music swells, then fades into silence.
Narrator (echoing, fading):
"And never let them erase the voices of the past."
